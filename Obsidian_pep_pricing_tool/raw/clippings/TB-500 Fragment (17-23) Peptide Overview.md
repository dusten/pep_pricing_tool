## Peptide Overview

TB-500 Fragment (17-23), commonly designated as fequesetide or by its amino acid sequence Ac-LKKTETQ, is a synthetic seven-amino acid peptide derived from the actin-binding domain of the naturally occurring protein thymosin beta-4 (TB4). While full-length thymosin beta-4 consists of 43 amino acids found abundantly in platelets, wound fluid, and various mammalian tissues, TB-500 Fragment represents an artificially synthesized and N-terminal acetylated extract of the internal active region responsible for actin sequestration, cell migration, and tissue repair. This fragment emerged as a research compound in veterinary and laboratory contexts beginning in the early 2000s, developed to isolate and study the most biologically potent region of thymosin beta-4 while potentially improving stability and bioavailability compared to the parent peptide.

Preclinical research spanning multiple decades has demonstrated that TB-500 Fragment (17-23) potently enhances wound healing through orchestrated effects on cell migration, angiogenesis, and inflammation modulation. Animal studies have consistently revealed that this fragment accelerates tissue repair in skin, corneal, cardiac, and musculoskeletal tissues, while simultaneously promoting blood vessel formation and reducing inflammatory markers. The peptide's mechanism centers on its direct binding to globular actin (G-actin) monomers within cells, thereby modulating cytoskeletal dynamics and enabling enhanced cellular motility toward injury sites. Despite accumulating preclinical evidence, TB-500 Fragment (17-23) remains a research chemical without FDA approval for human therapeutic use, and clinical translation has been limited.

## Research Findings

**Actin Sequestration and Cytoskeletal Dynamics**

**Mechanism of G-Actin Binding and Fibroblast Migration**

TB-500 Fragment (17-23) and its parent peptide thymosin beta-4 exert primary biological effects through direct binding to globular (G) actin monomers within cells. The LKKTETQ amino acid motif anchors specifically to the cleft region of G-actin, preventing premature polymerization into filamentous (F) actin and maintaining a cytoplasmic pool of sequestered actin monomers available for rapid cellular reorganization. This actin-sequestering function acts as a cellular buffer for actin homeostasis, allowing cells to rapidly remodel their cytoskeleton in response to signals for migration during wound closure and during endocytotic trafficking. Unlike F-actin (filamentous actin), TB-500 Fragment does not bind to or disrupt existing actin filaments, suggesting its primary role involves modulating actin monomer availability and polymerization kinetics rather than severing existing structures. This selective binding to G-actin but not F-actin represents a fundamental distinction that enables the peptide to fine-tune rather than dramatically disrupt cellular architecture.

**Cell Migration and Accelerated Wound Healing in Full-Thickness Skin Wounds**

- Study ([PMID: 10469335](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10469335/)): Full-thickness rat skin wound model with topical or intraperitoneal TB-500 (thymosin beta-4 from which TB-500 Fragment derives) administration; measurement of reepithelialization, wound contraction, fibroblast migration in Boyden chamber assay, and collagen deposition.
	- Findings: Addition of TB-500 topically or intraperitoneally increased reepithelialization by 42% over saline controls at 4 days post-wounding and by 61% at 7 days post-wounding. Treated wounds also contracted at least 11% more than controls by day 7. Increased collagen deposition and angiogenesis were observed in the treated wounds. TB-500 stimulated keratinocyte migration 2-3-fold over migration with medium alone when as little as 10 pg of TB-500 was added to the Boyden chamber assay, demonstrating remarkable dose-dependent potency.

**Improved Wound Healing in Diabetic and Aged Mice**

- Study ([PMID: 12581423](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12581423/)): Comparative assessment of thymosin beta-4 in models of impaired healing—full-thickness dermal wounds in db/db diabetic and aged mice; measurement of wound contracture, collagen deposition, and keratinocyte migration; testing of both PBS and hydrogel formulations; evaluation of the actin-binding peptide fragment LKKTETQ (TB-500 Fragment 17-23 equivalent).
	- Findings: In diabetic mice, wound contracture and collagen deposition were significantly increased in mice treated with thymosin beta-4 in either phosphate-buffered saline solution or hydrogel formulation. The actin-binding domain fragment LKKTETQ duplicated in a seven-amino acid synthetic peptide was able to promote repair in aged animals (26 months old) comparable to that observed with the parent molecule, demonstrating efficacy of TB-500 Fragment (17-23) in models of severely impaired healing.

**Angiogenesis and Pro-Angiogenic Mechanisms**

**Angiogenic Activity via Actin-Binding Motif**

- Study ([PMID: 14500546](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14500546/)): Investigation of the angiogenic mechanism of thymosin beta-4 using naturally occurring thymosin beta-4, proteolytic fragments, and synthetic peptides to identify the critical angiogenic domain; migration assays with human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs); vessel sprouting assays using chick aortic arches; adhesion assays.
	- Findings: A seven-amino acid actin-binding motif of thymosin beta-4 (LKKTETQ—the TB-500 Fragment 17-23 sequence) was essential for angiogenic activity. Thymosin beta-4 and the actin-binding motif displayed near-identical angiogenic activity at approximately 50 nM concentration, whereas peptides lacking portions of the actin motif were inactive. Migration assays, tube formation assays, and aortic ring sprouting assays all demonstrated that TB-500 Fragment (17-23) recapitulates the full angiogenic activity of the parent thymosin beta-4 molecule. Adhesion to thymosin beta-4 was blocked by the seven-amino acid peptide (TB-500 Fragment 17-23), demonstrating it as the major cell binding site on the molecule. The angiogenic activity of TB-500 Fragment was inhibited with addition of soluble actin, confirming actin-dependence of the mechanism.

**Endothelial Progenitor Cell (EPC) Angiogenesis via VEGF Signaling**

- Study ([PMID: 29956769](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29956769/)): Investigation of thymosin beta-4 effects on endothelial progenitor cell-mediated angiogenesis in ischemic myocardium; tube formation assay to assess angiogenesis; ELISA measurement of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) expression; assessment of Akt/eNOS signaling pathway via siRNA knockdown of Akt and eNOS.
	- Findings: Thymosin beta-4 improved the paracrine effects of EPCs, evidenced by increased VEGF expression. EPC-conditioned medium (EPC-CM) significantly promoted human umbilical vein endothelial cell angiogenesis in vitro, which was further enhanced by pretreatment with thymosin beta-4. The effect of TB-4 pretreated EPC-CM on angiogenesis was abolished by VEGF neutralizing antibody, indicating that increased VEGF secretion was pivotal. Akt/eNOS pathway involvement was confirmed when expression levels of VEGF in EPC-CM were significantly decreased following knockdown of Akt or eNOS by small interfering RNA transfection, indicating the Akt/eNOS signal transduction pathway’s role in TB-4-induced VEGF secretion.

**Capillary Formation and Vessel Growth in Cardiac Tissue**

- Study ([PMID: 20536454](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20536454/)): Assessment of thymosin beta-4 effects in mouse models of cardiac injury via myocardial infarction; measurement of vessel density, capillary number, and blood flow recovery; immunohistochemical analysis with alpha-smooth muscle actin (α-SMA) and PECAM-1 antibodies; investigation of growth factor expression (VEGF, Flk-1, FGF-17, FGFR-2, FGFR-4, TGF-β, β-Catenin).
	- Findings: Thymosin beta-4 initiated cardiac vessel formation in vivo, significantly increasing capillary number at the scar margin and in remote (non-infarcted) heart regions 3 days after treatment. Immunohistochemical analysis revealed increased capillary density marked by α-SMA and PECAM-1 positive cells. Systemic thymosin beta-4 injection altered expression of proteins essential for embryonic coronary development, including marked upregulation of VEGF, Flk-1, FGF-17, FGFR-2, FGFR-4, TGF-β, and β-Catenin expression notably in the thickened epicardium. These findings demonstrate that TB-500 Fragment-derived mechanisms promote multifactorial vascular growth critical to tissue regeneration.

**Wound Healing and Reepithelialization**

**Accelerated Corneal Epithelial Repair with Anti-Inflammatory Effects**

- Study ([PMID: 20536468](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20536468/)): Investigation of thymosin beta-4 effects in multiple models of corneal injury, including mechanical injury, chemical burns, and steroid-impaired healing; measurement of epithelial defect size, inflammatory markers (chemokines MIP-2 and KC), and corneal clarity over time.
	- Findings: Thymosin beta-4 demonstrated potent wound healing and anti-inflammatory effects in corneal epithelial defects. The peptide suppressed chemokine production (reduced corneal expression of PMN chemoattractants MIP-2 and KC) while promoting epithelial cell migration and barrier repair, maintaining corneal transparency even in steroid-treated animals that normally exhibit severely impaired healing. Topical thymosin beta-4 treatment after alkali injury down-regulated the expression of potent polymorphonuclear leukocyte (PMN) chemoattractants, suggesting mechanisms of reduced corneal inflammation and preserved healing capacity.

**Cutaneous Wound Healing in Full-Thickness Skin Defect**

- Study ([PMID: 17923415](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17923415/)): Recombinant human thymosin beta-4 expressed in Escherichia coli system; evaluation of biological activity via cell proliferation assay with spleen cells and wound assay using mouse (BALB/c) model; measurement of lymphocyte proliferation and full-thickness cutaneous wound healing.
	- Findings: Recombinant human thymosin beta-4 promoted lymphocyte proliferation and differentiation. Full-thickness cutaneous wound healing in BALB/c mice was significantly accelerated by recombinant TB-4 treatment, representing the first published report of recombinant human TB-4 demonstrating wound healing promotion. This validates that the wound-healing activities observed with TB-500 Fragment derive from the active TB4 core sequence.

**Hair Follicle Development and Growth**

**Hair Growth Promotion in Normal, Aged, and Steroid-Treated Rodents**

- Study ([PMID: 15037013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15037013/)): Assessment of thymosin beta-4 effects on hair follicle development in normal, aged, and steroid-treated rodents following TB-500 administration; measurement of hair growth rates, follicle size, and angiogenic support to hair-bearing skin; examination of angiogenesis, wound healing, and hair growth in aged animals where growth is normally diminished.
	- Findings: Thymosin beta-4 promoted hair follicle growth in both normal and aged rodents, with enhanced hair growth observed particularly in aging models where growth is normally diminished. The mechanisms involved both enhanced angiogenesis to the hair follicle region and direct effects on stem cell migration within the follicular compartment. These findings suggest utility of TB-500 Fragment in aging-related hair loss and regeneration contexts through the peptide’s actin-binding activity facilitating stem cell mobilization.

**Anti-Inflammatory and Immunomodulatory Effects**

**Suppression of Inflammatory Cytokine Signaling and Anti-Osteoclastogenesis**

- Study ([PMID: 26789270](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26789270/)): Investigation of thymosin beta-4 expression in H₂O₂-stimulated human periodontal ligament cells (PDLCs) and effects on inflammatory response and osteoclastic differentiation in mouse bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMMs); measurement of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, IL-17), inflammatory mediators (nitric oxide, prostaglandin E2), and MAPK signaling (ERK, JNK, p38); assessment of osteoclast-specific gene expression (cathepsin K, calcitonin receptor, NFATc1).
	- Findings: Thymosin beta-4 peptide attenuated H₂O₂-induced production of NO and PGE₂ and suppressed upregulation of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, IL-17). The peptide inhibited activation of ERK and JNK MAPK as well as NF-κB, a central transcription factor governing inflammatory gene expression. Thymosin beta-4 inhibited osteoclastic differentiation and reduced osteoclast-specific gene expression. These anti-inflammatory effects via TB4 suggest that TB-500 Fragment (17-23), which contains the active actin-binding domain, exhibits similar immunomodulatory properties and protective effects against inflammatory osteolytic diseases such as periodontitis.

**Mast Cell Mediator Release**

- Study ([PMID: 17289217](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17289217/)): Investigation of thymosin beta-4 effects on mediator release from murine peritoneal and human HMC-1 mast cells; exposure to 0.2-2000 nM concentrations of thymosin beta-4 and peptide fragments including the actin-binding motif (amino acids 17-23, LKKTETQ); measurement of mast cell degranulation and mediator (histamine, tryptase, β-hexosaminidase) release.
	- Findings: Thymosin beta-4 induced dose-dependent mediator release (up to 23%) in both murine peritoneal and human HMC-1 mast cells. Critically, peptides corresponding to thymosin beta-4 amino acid sequences 16-38 and 17-23 (TB-500 Fragment) stimulated mast cell mediator release on a level equal to or higher than native thymosin beta-4. These observations suggest that the actin-binding motif LKKTETQ (amino acids 17-22 of TB4, present in TB-500 Fragment 17-23) is implicated in mast cell activation, potentially important for understanding TB-500 Fragment mechanisms in vivo through mast cell-mediated inflammatory modulation and tissue repair signaling.

**Cardiac Tissue Regeneration**

**Myocardial Cell Survival and Epicardial Activation**

- Study ([PMID: 20536454](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20536454/)): Investigation of thymosin beta-4 effects in adult mouse hearts following myocardial infarction via left anterior descending (LAD) coronary artery ligation; measurement of myocardial cell death inhibition at 24 hours post-infarction, epicardial thickening, cardiac progenitor cell activation; immunohistochemical analysis of myocardial progenitor markers (Tbx-18, Wt-1 positive cells); assessment of vascular density and endothelial regeneration.
	- Findings: Thymosin beta-4 inhibited myocardial cell death at 24 hours after cardiac ligation, minimizing cardiomyocyte loss after cardiac infarction. The peptide stimulated vessel growth, increased or activated proteins and epicardial progenitors important for cardiac repair, and activated endogenous cardiac progenitors by inducing the adult heart to recapitulate its embryonic developmental program in vivo. Initiation of epicardial thickening accompanied by increase of myocardial and epicardial progenitors with or without infarction indicated that the reactivation process was independent of injury. These results demonstrate thymosin beta-4 (and its active TB-500 Fragment 17-23 domain) to be a molecule capable of initiating simultaneous myocardial and vascular regeneration after systemic administration.

**PKC Activation and Embryonic Gene Program Reactivation**

- Study ([PMID: 20536454](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20536454/)): Microarray analysis (Mouse Genome 430 2.0 Affymetrix cDNA microarrays) of adult mouse hearts after thymosin beta-4 administration; RT-PCR validation of gene expression changes; Western blot analysis of signaling molecules; immunohistochemical analysis of Marcks (myristoylated alanine-rich C-kinase substrate) expression and phosphorylation; investigation of PKC substrate phosphorylation as indicator of PKC activity.
	- Findings: cDNA microarray identified 2.8-fold upregulation of Marcks (a prominent intracellular substrate for protein kinase C, PKC) after thymosin beta-4 administration. Real-time RT-PCR confirmed microarray results. Immunocytochemistry of human coronary endothelial cells (HCECs) demonstrated increased Marcks expression and translocation from the cell membrane to the cytosol after thymosin beta-4 treatment, indicating PKC activation. These molecular changes in epicardial gene expression suggest that thymosin beta-4 (and TB-500 Fragment 17-23) activates PKC signaling essential for epicardial cell transformation, vascular progenitor differentiation, and angiogenic vessel formation during cardiac regeneration.

**Collagen Organization and Connective Tissue Repair**

**Organized Collagen Deposition and Wound Maturation**

- Study ([PMID: 20536458](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20536458/)): Investigation of thymosin beta-4 effects on dermal wound healing and connective tissue repair in rat incisional wound model; local application of thymosin beta-4; measurement of wound strength, scar formation, collagen organization via histological analysis (Masson staining), and mechanical tensile strength testing.
	- Findings: Incisional wounds in rats treated locally with thymosin beta-4 healed with minimal scarring and without loss in wound breaking strength. The peptide organized connective tissue repair, facilitating both rapid healing and functional restoration. These findings suggest that TB-500 Fragment (17-23) similarly promotes organized collagen deposition and minimizes scar formation through its effects on fibroblast migration and extracellular matrix remodeling.

**Metabolic Fate and Bioavailability**

**Detection in Plasma and Urine Following Administration**

- Study ([PMID: 23084823](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23084823/)): Liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC/MS) analysis of N-acetylated LKKTETQ (TB-500) and its metabolites in equine urine and plasma following administration; development of detection methods for sports doping control.
	- Findings: The parent TB-500 peptide (N-acetyl-LKKTETQ) could be detected at 0.02 ng/mL in equine plasma and 0.01 ng/mL in equine urine following a single 10 mg intramuscular dose. Metabolite analysis identified peptide fragmentation products, indicating enzymatic breakdown of the peptide both in vivo and in biological samples. The identification of TB-500 and metabolites represents the first characterization of the peptide’s metabolic fate in post-administration biological samples, providing insight into the peptide’s absorption, circulation, and elimination kinetics.

**Important Research Considerations**

**Structural Distinctions and Their Functional Implications**

Research utilizing TB-500 Fragment (17-23) must carefully distinguish this compound from full-length TB-500, as the two exhibit different chemical formulas, molecular weights, and potentially different bioavailability profiles. While both share the identical seven-amino acid LKKTETQ sequence, TB-500 Fragment lacks the aldehyde modification present in TB-500, conferring superior chemical stability and potentially an extended biological half-life. The implications are that studies comparing TB-500 and TB-500 Fragment (17-23) may demonstrate different potency or duration of effects despite their identical amino acid sequences.

**Dose-Dependent Effects and Remarkable Potency**

Preclinical studies demonstrate that TB-500 Fragment and its parent peptide thymosin beta-4 exhibit remarkable biological potency at extremely low concentrations. For example, in fibroblast and keratinocyte migration assays, effects were observed with as little as 10 picograms of the peptide, suggesting extraordinarily high specific activity. This extreme potency raises important questions about the biological relevance of various reported dose ranges in the literature and suggests that optimal research dosing may be considerably lower than commonly assumed.

**Actin-Binding Motif as Essential Domain**

The seven-amino acid actin-binding motif LKKTETQ is essential for the angiogenic and wound-healing properties of thymosin beta-4. Synthetic peptides containing the complete motif recapitulate the full biological activity of the parent molecule, whereas peptides lacking any portion of the motif are inactive. This validates TB-500 Fragment (17-23) as a legitimate active extract representing the core functional domain of thymosin beta-4.

**Limited Human Clinical Evidence**

While preclinical animal studies of thymosin beta-4 and related fragments have demonstrated consistent healing effects, human clinical evidence remains limited. Phase 2 clinical trials for thymosin beta-4 have been conducted in patients with epidermolysis bullosa, pressure sores (pressure ulcers, stage III/IV), and venous stasis ulcers. Results revealed that thymosin beta-4 accelerated healing by approximately one month in patients who achieved complete wound closure, with approximately 25% of patients healing completely within 3 months (particularly those with small to moderate wounds). However, TB-500 Fragment (17-23) specifically has not been extensively evaluated in human clinical trials, representing a critical gap in evidence supporting therapeutic claims.

**Regulatory Status**

TB-500 Fragment (17-23) is not approved by the FDA for human therapeutic use and is prohibited as a dietary supplement ingredient. The compound has been detected in misuse contexts in equine sports and veterinary medicine, which has driven development of detection and doping control methods. All applications of TB-500 Fragment remain restricted to research contexts in laboratory and preclinical animal model systems.
