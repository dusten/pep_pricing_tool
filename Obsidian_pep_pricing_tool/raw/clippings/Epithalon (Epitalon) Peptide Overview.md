## Peptide Overview

Epithalon (also known as epitalon or epithalone) is a synthetic tetrapeptide consisting of four amino acids with the sequence Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly (AEDG), developed in the late 1980s by Professor Vladimir Khavinson and colleagues at the Saint Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology in Russia. Epithalon was rationally designed as a synthetic analog of epithalamin, a naturally occurring peptide preparation extracted from bovine pineal gland tissue, which had demonstrated bioactive properties in earlier studies. The peptide's primary mechanism of action involves upregulation of telomerase, a ribonucleoprotein enzyme that maintains and extends telomere length by synthesizing telomeric DNA sequences, thereby counteracting the progressive telomere shortening that occurs with cellular division and aging.

Preclinical research spanning over three decades has established that epithalon possesses remarkable geroprotective (anti-aging), antioxidant, and pleiotropic biological activities. In animal models, the peptide increases both average lifespan and maximum lifespan, reduces chromosomal aberrations, delays the onset of age-related diseases, and suppresses development of certain malignancies including leukemia. More recently, quantitative molecular studies have documented that epithalon induces dose-dependent telomere elongation in human somatic cells through hTERT (human telomerase reverse transcriptase) upregulation and telomerase enzyme activation, while paradoxically activating alternative lengthening of telomeres (ALT) specifically in cancer cells, suggesting context-dependent cellular responses.

Additionally, epithalon restores melatonin secretion from the pineal gland in aging subjects and has been documented to normalize disturbed circadian rhythms of neurotransmitters disrupted by xenobiotic exposure, supporting its classification as both an endocrine regulator and a circadian chronobiotic agent. The peptide demonstrates tissue-specific activity, with particularly pronounced effects on pineal gland function and selective protection of aged pineal cells from further senescence. Human clinical trials conducted primarily in Russian research institutions have reported modest improvements in telomere length, reduced cardiovascular and all-cause mortality in elderly populations when combined with other bioregulatory peptides, and high efficacy (90%) in treating retinitis pigmentosa when administered systemically.

## Chemical Identification

**Common Names:** Epithalon, Epitalon, Epithalone, Epithalamin (for the natural pineal extract), Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly, AEDG tetrapeptide, H-Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly-OH

**CAS Number:** 307297-39-8 (primary); also 64082-79-7 (alternate designation)

**Molecular Formula:** C₁₄H₂₂N₄O₉ (for free acid form); C₁₆H₂₆N₄O₁₁ for acetate salt form

**Molecular Weight:** 390.35 g/mol (free acid); 450.4 g/mol (acetate salt)

**Origin & Type Classification:**

- **Source:** Synthetic; rationally designed based on epithalamin, a naturally occurring peptide from bovine pineal gland
- **Biosynthesis:** Non-ribosomal; chemically synthesized through peptide bond formation
- **Functional Class:** Telomerase activator; geroprotective peptide; neuroendocrine modulator; anti-aging bioregulator

**Additional Information:**

- **Amino Acid Sequence:** N-Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly-C (single-letter code: AEDG; where A = alanine, E = glutamic acid, D = aspartic acid, G = glycine)
- **Sequence Length:** 4 amino acids (tetrapeptide)
- **Structural Type:** Linear peptide; amphiphilic structure containing both hydrophobic (alanine) and hydrophilic (acidic residues) amino acids
- **Salt Form:** Commonly available as acetate salt; also exists as trifluoroacetate or hydrochloride forms in research preparations
- **Key Structural Features:** The tetrapeptide sequence is highly conserved in research literature; appears to represent the bioactive core of the longer epithalamin polypeptide preparation
- **Known Synonyms:** Epitalon, Epithalon, Epithalone, Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly, H-AEDG-OH, Epithalon tetrapeptide
- **Supplier Identification Variations:** UNII code 65IP17785G; ChEBI ID CHEBI:230091; DrugBank ID DB17882

**Database Links:**

- **PubChem:** CID 219042 (Epitalon)
- **UniProt:** No specific entry; Epithalon is a synthetic peptide not corresponding to a full-length natural mammalian protein
- **PDB:** No experimental structural entry available as of October 2025
- **NCBI:** Extensive literature accessible through PubMed; searched term “epithalon aging” returns 200+ publications

**Note:** Chemical identifiers are consistent across major suppliers. The free acid form (C₁₄H₂₂N₄O₉, MW 390.35) represents the core peptide, while pharmaceutical formulations typically employ the acetate salt (MW 450.4 g/mol) for improved stability and solubility.

## Research Findings

**Telomerase Activation and Telomere Length Extension**

**Dose-Dependent hTERT Upregulation in Normal Human Cells**

- **Study ([PMID: 40908429](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40908429/)):** Normal human epithelial and fibroblast cells treated with escalating epithalon concentrations; qPCR analysis of hTERT (human telomerase reverse transcriptase) mRNA; immunofluorescence and quantitative PCR measurement of telomere length.
	- **Findings:** Epithalon produced dose-dependent increases in hTERT mRNA expression. Concurrent with hTERT upregulation, telomere length extended significantly in a concentration-dependent manner in both epithelial and fibroblast cell types. Telomerase enzyme activity increased proportionally with hTERT expression, demonstrating a clear mechanistic link between the peptide’s induction of the telomerase catalytic subunit and resulting telomere maintenance.

**Alternative Lengthening of Telomeres (ALT) Activation in Cancer Cells**

- **Study ([PMID: 40908429](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40908429/)):** Breast cancer cell lines (21NT and BT474) treated with epithalon; telomere length, hTERT expression, telomerase activity, and ALT activity measured via qPCR and immunofluorescence.
	- **Findings:** Cancer cells demonstrated significant telomere length extension following epithalon treatment, but through a mechanistically distinct pathway: ALT (alternative lengthening of telomeres) activation. This pathway, which occurs independently of telomerase, involves homologous recombination-mediated telomere elongation. Only minor ALT activity was detected in normal cells, indicating that epithalon’s ALT-activating effect is cancer-cell-specific, whereas hTERT/telomerase activation occurs in normal cells.

**Human Fibroblast Telomere Elongation**

- **Study:** Cultured human fetal fibroblasts exposed to epithalon at physiologically relevant concentrations.
	- **Findings:** Telomere length increased by 33% in epithalon-treated fibroblasts compared to untreated controls. This 33% telomere elongation represents a measurable extension of cellular replicative lifespan capacity, as telomere length directly correlates with the number of additional cell divisions possible before reaching senescence (the “Hayflick limit”).

**Longevity and Lifespan Extension in Animal Models**

**Extended Lifespan in Multiple Mouse Strains**

- **Study ([PMID: 14501183](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14501183/)):** Female Swiss-derived SHR mice treated from age 3 months until natural death with subcutaneous epithalon injections (1.0 μg/mouse, approximately 30-40 μg/kg) on 5 consecutive days each month; 54 mice per group.
	- **Findings:** Mean lifespan was not significantly altered by epithalon treatment. However, the maximum lifespan increased by 12.3% compared to controls, and notably, the lifespan of the last 10% of surviving animals (the longest-lived cohort) increased by 13.3% (P<0.01). This pattern is characteristic of a geroprotective agent that preferentially extends the lifespan of individuals with inherent longevity potential.

**Extended Lifespan in CBA Mice**

- **Study:** Long-term study on CBA mice strain receiving monthly epithalon treatments over 21 months.
	- **Findings:** At 23 months of age, the number of epithalon-treated mice exceeding this age was 4.0-fold higher than in untreated controls (P<0.01). The oldest saline control mice died at 24 months, while the oldest epithalon-treated mice survived to 34 months of age. This 10-month extension in maximum lifespan in treated animals represents remarkable longevity enhancement in a commonly-used laboratory strain.

**HER-2/neu Transgenic Mouse Model**

- **Study:** Transgenic mice overexpressing HER-2/neu (an oncogene associated with aggressive cancers); treated with epithalon in two different schemes.
	- **Findings:** Depending on dosage regimen, epithalon either enhanced or reduced maximum lifespan. At appropriate doses, epithalon significantly enhanced survival rates through increased average robustness and heterogeneity in the population. This dose-sensitivity suggests biphasic effects where optimal epithalon concentrations confer survival advantages while suboptimal or excessive doses may create metabolic stress.

**Chromosomal Protection and Antimutagenic Activity**

**Reduced Chromosomal Aberrations in Aging**

- **Study ([PMID: 14501183](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14501183/)):** Bone marrow cells from treated and untreated mice assessed for frequency of chromosome aberrations.
	- **Findings:** Epithalon decreased the frequency of chromosomal aberrations by 17.1% (P<0.05) compared to controls. Since chromosomal aberrations accumulate with aging and are markers of genomic instability, this reduction suggests epithalon maintains genomic integrity through antioxidant and DNA-protective mechanisms.

**Antimutagenic Activity in Hair Color-Related Models**

- **Study:** Albino and grey mice assessed for sperm head abnormalities and micronuclei in peripheral blood erythrocytes following epithalon treatment.
	- **Findings:** Epithalon demonstrated significant antimutagenic action (protection against mutations) in both assays using grey mice and enhanced antimutagenic activity in both tests with albino mice. The apparent color-dependence of results suggests potential differences in melanin-related protective mechanisms or oxidative stress resilience between pigmented and non-pigmented individuals.

**Radioprotection Against Ionizing Radiation**

- **Study:** Wistar rats subjected to gamma (γ) irradiation; epithalon administered intraperitoneally on days 2-6 post-irradiation.
	- **Findings:** Epithalon reduced apoptosis (programmed cell death) of splenic lymphocytes by 2.12-fold (P<0.05) 2 days post-irradiation. The peptide also partially preserved pineal gland structure from radiation-induced damage and facilitated recovery, particularly restoring mitochondrial ultrastructure. This radioprotective effect may relate to epithalon’s pineal tropism and anti-apoptotic mechanisms.

**Cancer Inhibition and Tumor Suppression**

**Leukemia Suppression**

- **Study ([PMID: 14501183](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14501183/)):** Female SHR mice monitored for spontaneous tumor development over their entire lifespan with epithalon or placebo treatment.
	- **Findings:** Total spontaneous tumor incidence was not significantly altered by epithalon treatment. However, the incidence of leukemia (blood cancers) was dramatically reduced by 6.0-fold (P<0.05) in treated animals compared to controls. This selective suppression of leukemia without affecting other cancers suggests epithalon may modulate hematopoietic stem cell function or blood cell homeostasis through tissue-specific mechanisms.

**Anti-Neoplastic Activity in Retinitis Pigmentosa Patients**

- **Study:** Human clinical trial in 45 patients with retinitis pigmentosa (a degenerative eye disease with potential malignant transformation of photoreceptors); systemic epithalon administered.
	- **Findings:** Epithalon produced a positive clinical effect in 90% of treated subjects, with improvements in vision, reduced photoreceptor degeneration, and decreased risk of malignant transformation. The remarkable efficacy (90% positive response rate) in this neoplasia-related retinal condition suggests epithalon’s anti-neoplastic properties extend beyond systemic cancers to organ-specific malignant processes.

**Melatonin Restoration and Circadian Rhythm Normalization**

**Restoration of Nocturnal Melatonin Secretion in Aging Humans and Primates**

- **Study:** Aged humans (60-80 years) and aged primates with suppressed nighttime melatonin levels; epithalamin (parent extract) or epithalon administered over repeated treatment courses; 24-hour circulating melatonin levels measured.
	- **Findings:** Both epithalamin and epithalon significantly increased nocturnal melatonin secretion toward levels observed in younger individuals, with efficacy comparable between the two preparations. Circadian phase and amplitude of melatonin rhythms normalized, supporting the peptide’s capacity to restore youthful pineal gland function and circadian chronobiology.

**Neurotransmitter Circadian Rhythm Restoration Following Xenobiotic Exposure**

- **Study ([PMID: 23237594](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23237594/)):** Female rats exposed to 1,2-dimethylhydrazine (DMH), a neurotoxic xenobiotic causing reproductive system premature aging; epithalamin or epithalon administered; diurnal dynamics of norepinephrine (NE) and dopamine (DA) measured in critical hypothalamic regions.
	- **Findings:** DMH administration disrupted normal circadian rhythms of catecholamines in the medial preoptic area (MPA) and median eminence-arcuate nuclei (ME-Arc). Epithalamin recovered diurnal NE dynamics in the MPA to control patterns. Epitalon proved more effective in the ME-Arc, preventing the xenobiotic-induced disturbance of DA circadian rhythm and preserving the typical low DA pattern at 5:00 CT with elevation by 11:00 CT. These findings suggest epithalon protects circadian gonadotropin-releasing hormone surge timing from xenobiotic disruption.

**Antioxidant and Anti-Apoptotic Effects**

**Free Radical Suppression in Brain and Liver**

- **Study:** CBA mice treated chronically with epithalon; lipid peroxidation (LPO) rates measured in brain and serum.
	- **Findings:** Epithalon significantly suppressed free radical generation and lipid peroxidation in brain tissue. This reduction in oxidative stress markers indicates enhanced antioxidant defenses, particularly critical in the lipid-rich central nervous system where oxidative damage accelerates neurodegeneration.

**Lysosomal Membrane Stabilization in Aging Tissues**

- **Study:** Aging rats (2-24 months old) receiving monthly epithalon courses (5 consecutive daily injections of 100 μg/kg); lysosomal integrity assessed in brain, heart, and liver tissues.
	- **Findings:** Epithalon stabilized lysosomal membranes throughout the lifespan, accompanied by increased intensity of lysosomal proteolysis. By preserving lysosomal integrity, epithalon prevents uncontrolled release of hydrolytic enzymes that damage cellular components during aging and ischemic stress, representing a cellular-level anti-aging mechanism.

**Wound Healing and Tissue Repair**

**Accelerated Wound Closure in High-Glucose Retinal Cells**

- **Study:** Human retinal endothelial cells cultured in high-glucose media (simulating diabetic hyperglycemia, a model of excessive oxidative stress); epithalon administered; wound closure assay performed.
	- **Findings:** Epithalon accelerated wound closure and promoted cell migration in this harsh oxidative environment. The peptide reduced reactive oxygen species (ROS) accumulation and suppressed fibrosis-related signaling, demonstrating tissue-repair properties particularly relevant to diabetic complications and chronic inflammatory conditions.

**Neuronal and Developmental Protection**

**Protection of Oocytes from Post-Ovulatory Aging Damage**

- **Study ([PMID: 338](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/338/)):** Mouse oocytes (eggs) aged *in vitro*; epithalon administered; spindle apparatus morphology and cortical granule distribution assessed.
	- **Findings:** Epithalon treatment significantly decreased the frequency of spindle defects and abnormal cortical granule distribution during aging. Since oocyte quality declines with female reproductive aging, epithalon’s protection of these critical structures suggests potential applicability to female reproductive aging and age-associated subfertility.

**Neuronal Differentiation Enhancement in Stem Cells**

- **Study:** Human gingival mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) treated with epithalon; RT-PCR analysis of neurogenic markers.
	- **Findings:** Epithalon significantly increased transcription of Growth Associated Protein 43 (GAP43) and Nestin (P<0.01), markers of neuronal differentiation and neurite growth. Additional increases in β-tubulin III and Doublecortin mRNA were observed, indicating epithalon promotes neurogenic differentiation of MSCs, with potential implications for peripheral nerve regeneration and neurodegenerative disease models.

**Endocrine and Reproductive System Effects**

**Delayed Age-Related Loss of Estrous Cycles**

- **Study ([PMID: 14501183](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14501183/)):** Female SHR mice treated chronically with epithalon from age 3 months; estrous cycling patterns monitored over 21+ months.
	- **Findings:** Epithalon slowed the age-related switching-off of estrous function. While not preventing reproductive aging entirely, the peptide delayed the transition to anestrus (permanent cessation of estrous cycles), extending the reproductive window. This finding aligns with epithalon’s documented effects on circadian gonadotropin-releasing hormone signaling and neuroendocrine function.

**Physical Activity and Behavior**

**Biphasic Effects on Locomotor Activity**

- **Study:** CBA mice receiving long-term epithalon treatment assessed for physical activity over 21 months.
	- **Findings:** During the first 3 months of epithalon administration, physical activity decreased significantly (P<0.001), suggesting an initial metabolic adjustment period. However, this parameter normalized by later timepoints and did not differ significantly from controls by study end. Muscular strength and physical fatigability were unaffected throughout the study, indicating the initial decrease in activity represents a transient adaptive response rather than a sustained debilitative effect.

**Human Clinical Evidence of Mortality Reduction**

**Cardiovascular Mortality Reduction in Long-Term Prospective Studies**

- **Study:** Prospective cohort study of 79 coronary artery disease patients followed for >12 years; epithalamin (parent peptide preparation) administered biennially for 3 years; cardiovascular and metabolic endpoints assessed.
	- **Findings:** Treated patients demonstrated: 50% lower cardiovascular mortality, 50% lower serious cardiovascular and respiratory disease rates, and 28% lower all-cause mortality compared to controls. Additionally, improvements in physical endurance, circadian rhythm parameters, and carbohydrate/lipid metabolism were documented. These clinical findings provide human evidence supporting epithalon’s therapeutic potential in age-related cardiovascular disease.

**All-Cause Mortality Reduction in Elderly Population**

- **Study:** Prospective cohort study of 266 community-dwelling people over age 60; single epithalamin administration; 6-year mortality follow-up.
	- **Findings:** Epithalamin alone produced a 1.6–1.8-fold reduction in 6-year mortality. When combined with thymalin (a thymus peptide), mortality reduction increased to 2.5-fold. When combined with thymalin and administered annually (rather than as single dose), mortality reduction reached 4.1-fold. These data demonstrate synergistic geroprotective effects when epithalon is combined with other bioregulatory peptides and provided at optimized dosing intervals.

**Note on Clinical Status and Research Limitations:** Epithalon has undergone extensive investigation in Russian and Eastern European research institutions, with most clinical data originating from cohort studies conducted at the Saint Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology. The peptide has not been approved by Western regulatory agencies (FDA, EMA) and remains classified as a research compound. While recent mechanistic studies from independent Western laboratories (Brunel University and others) have provided quantitative validation of epithalon’s telomerase-activating effects in human cells, large-scale randomized controlled clinical trials by Western regulatory standards have not been conducted. Most human clinical data originate from observational and retrospective cohort studies, necessitating cautious interpretation while acknowledging the compelling biological mechanisms and promising observational findings.
